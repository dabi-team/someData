Blockchain empowers a decentralized economy by enabling distributed trust in a peer-to-peer network.
However, surprisingly, a widely accepted definition or measurement of decentralization is still lacking. We
explore a systematization of knowledge (SoK) on blockchain decentralization by comprehensively analyzing
existing studies in various aspects. First, we establish a taxonomy for analyzing blockchain decentralization
in the five facets of consensus, network, governance, wealth, and transaction. We find a lack of research
on the transaction aspects that closely characterize user behavior. Second, we apply Shannon entropy in
information theory to propose a decentralization index for blockchain transactions. We show that our index
intuitively measures levels of decentralization in peer-to-peer transactions by simulating blockchain token
transfers. Third, we apply our index to empirically analyze the dynamics of DeFi token transfers by three
methods of description, prediction, and causal inference. In the descriptive analysis, we observe that levels of
decentralization converge inter-temporally, regardless of the initial levels. A comparative study across DeFi
applications shows that exchange and lending are more decentralized than payment and derivatives across
DeFi applications. Second, in the predictive analysis, we also discover that a greater return of Ether, the native
coin of the Ethereum blockchain, predicts a greater transaction decentralization in stablecoin that include
Ether as collateral. Third, in an event study of causal inference, we find the change of Ethereum Transaction
Fee Mechanism to EIP-1559 significantly changes the decentralization level of DeFi transactions. Finally,
we identify future research directions: 1) to explore the interactions between different facets of blockchain
decentralization, 2) to design blockchain mechanisms that achieve sustainable decentralization, and 3) to
study the interplay of decentralization levels and economic factors. We also raise challenges in addressing the
controversies in blockchain decentralization.

1 INTRODUCTION
Blockchain empowers finance by enabling an economy of decentralization [Harvey et al., 2021].
Decentralization is a core property of blockchain that enables system security and the promise of a
distributed peer-to-peer network [Budish, 2022, Puthal et al., 2018, Weyl et al., 2022]. Blockchain
and decentralized finance (DeFi) applications on blockchain promise to be non-custodial and openly
auditable [Gomber et al., 2017, SchÃ¤r, 2020, Werner et al., 2021]. However, the actual level of
decentralization varies greatly in blockchain applications [Campajola et al., 2022, Cong et al., 2022,
De Collibus et al., 2021, Gencer et al., 2018, Lin et al., 2021, Oliva et al., 2020]. Ao et al. [2022] even
identify a core-periphery structure in DeFi token transactions rather than a distributed network
that blockchain promises to provide. Moreover, a widely accepted definition or measurement of
decentralization is still lacking. In this paper, we explore a systematization of knowledge (SoK) [jsy,
[n. d.], sys, [n. d.]] on blockchain decentralization by answering the following three questions:

â€¢ RQ1 (establish the taxonomy of blockchain decentralization). What are the facets of

blockchain decentralization and can we measure the levels of decentralization?

â€¢ RQ2 (propose an explainable decentralization index). Can we propose a new index that
intuitively characterizes the decentralization level of blockchain transactions which is closely
connected to user behavior?

â€¢ RQ3 (apply the decentralization index to empirical research). How does the decentral-
ization of DeFi token transfers distribute and change, and interact with economic factors?

We first review the literature that investigates various aspects of decentralization in terms of the
blockchain layer impact and the method of measurement. By means of the comprehensive review,
we aim to establish a taxonomy of blockchain decentralization. Then, we seek to further expand
the understanding of decentralization to on-chain transactions that characterize user behavior. Are
transactions made by a few users in large amounts, or are they distributed across many users in
small amounts? Transaction decentralization also describes the usage of a blockchain network or
application. Is the blockchain used by many parties, or is usage concentrated? Moreover, how can we
measure the actual level of usage decentralization? Entropy [Shannon, 1948] in information theory
quantifies the amount of information carried by events and is used to determine the unevenness
of a probability distribution.1 By adapting entropy to measure the degree of randomness in the
distribution of transactions, we propose a new decentralized index as the entropy of transactions.
We test the explainability of the index via comparative statics [Dixit, 1986], which compares the
simulated decentralization index of various transaction distributions. Third, we apply our newly
proposed index to study the dynamics of the decentralization of DeFi token transfers. By analyzing
data from the Ethereum blockchain by three methods of description, prediction, and causal inference,
we explore how the decentralization level evolves intertemporally, differs across applications, and
interacts with economic factors.

We have three major findings (F) that answer the RQs.

(1) F1. We establish a comprehensive taxonomy for analyzing blockchain decentralization in
the five facets of consensus, network, governance, wealth, and transaction in terms of the
blockchain layer impacted. Both theoretical models of game theory and empirical measure-
ments, such as the Gini coefficient and Shannon entropy, have been applied to study the first
four facets of decentralization: consensus [Gochhayat et al., 2020], network [Gencer et al.,
2018], governance [Pelt et al., 2020], and wealth [Gupta and Gupta, 2018]. However, we find
a lack of research in the transaction aspects that closely characterize user behavior.

(2) F2. We propose a new index that intuitively characterizes the level of decentralization by
transforming Shannon entropy. Our index increases when the number of transactions in-
creases and when the distribution of transactions is more evenly distributed, intuitively
reflecting greater levels of decentralization.

(3) F3. By applying our proposed index to empirical research on top DeFi applications, we find
that the level of transaction decentralization varies greatly across different DeFi applica-
tions and within an application temporally in the descriptive analysis. First, decentralized
exchanges (DEXs) and lending applications appear slightly more decentralized overall than
payment and derivative applications. This is likely due to a greater demand for exchanging
and borrowing, leading to a greater number and more even distributions of transactions.
Intuitively, applications that become more popular and are utilized more tend to become more
decentralized over time, while applications that are utilized less become less decentralized
over time. Second, there appears to be a convergence in the level of intertemporal decentral-
ization overall. In the predictive analysis, we document interactions between the levels of
decentralization and economic factors. We discover that a greater return of ether, the native
coin of the Ethereum blockchain, predicts a greater decentralization level in stablecoins trans-
fers that includes ether as collateral. Lastly, we study how the change in economic mechanism
affects the level of decentralization. We discover a significant increase in decentralization in
response to Ethereum Improvement Protocol 1559 (EIP-1559), an essential modification to
the Ethereum blockchainâ€™s transaction fee mechanism.

2017] and security [Herrera-JoancomartÃ­ and PÃ©rez-SolÃ , 2016, Zhou et al., 2020], are desirable
properties of a blockchain ecosystem. However, Vitalik Butterin [Hafid et al., 2020] first described

each desirable property and understand its effect on the ecosystem. We contribute to the literature
from the aspect of decentralization and analyze the specific SoK topics.

distribution. The application layer consists of contract and application layers that include smart

blockchain system and all blockchain use cases, such as DeFi.

We contribute to the literature by further incorporating the aspects of blockchain decentraliza-
tion into the layered architecture. Consensus and network decentralization are connected to the
infrastructure layer, wealth decentralization is connected to the incentive layer, and governance
and transaction decentralization is connected to the application layer.

Li and Palanisamy [2020], Lin et al. [2021], Srinivasan [2017] and Wu et al. [2019] empirically
measure consensus decentralization using metrics such as the Gini coefficient, Shannon entropy,
and the Nakamoto coefficient. Arnosti and Weinberg [2022], Capponi et al. [2021], Chu and Wang
[2018], Cong et al. [2020], Eyal and Sirer [2018] and Gervais et al. [2014] theoretically examine the
decentralization of consensus protocols using methods such as game theory to model consensus
participation and equilibrium.
2.1.2 Network Decentralization. Gencer et al. [2018] describe network decentralization as the
fragmentation of control over the network, manifested in the security and fairness of a network
system. Network decentralization thus depicts decentralization in the underlying blockchain peer-
to-peer network infrastructure, such as how users communicate with the network and how nodes
communicate with each other. Gencer et al. [2018] examines network decentralization using prior
internet measurement techniques along with the geographical distribution of nodes. Lee et al.
[2021] also studies network decentralization through the geographical distribution of nodes.

2.1.3 Wealth Decentralization. Wealth decentralization is commonly defined as the evenness in
the distribution of wealth in the blockchain literature [Gupta and Gupta, 2018, Wu et al., 2019].
Wealth decentralization thus captures the decentralization of monetary assets in tokens and native
cryptocurrencies distributed across blockchain users. Gupta and Gupta [2018], Srinivasan [2017]
and Wu et al. [2019] analyze the wealth decentralization of native currencies on the Bitcoin and
Ethereum blockchains using metrics such as the Gini coefficient. [Cong et al., 2022] utilizes the
Herfindahlâ€“Hirschman Index (HHI) to describe the wealth decentralization of Ethereum.

2.1.4 Governance Decentralization. Governance [Pelt et al., 2020, Reijers et al., 2016] is often defined
as the processes of governing, whether undertaken by a government, market, or network, whether
over a family, tribe, formal or informal organization, or territory, and whether through laws, norms,
power or language. Blockchain governance [Gu et al., 2020, Lee et al., 2020, Markus, 2007, Pelt
et al., 2020] is often defined as the means of achieving the direction, control, and coordination of
stakeholders within the context of a given blockchain project to which they jointly contribute.
Governance decentralization is often defined as the extent to which control over the blockchain
is shared between platform owners and participants [Brummer, 2019, Chen et al., 2020, Faguet,
2014]. Ostrom [1990] describes a fully decentralized governance structure where platform users
collectively have full governance control and can represent their perspectives.

Governance decentralization thus specifies the decentralization of ownership and decision-
making power on blockchain platforms and how they are shared between owners and partici-
pants. Pelt et al. [2020] define a framework to examine blockchain governance and aspects of
governance decentralization. Chen et al. [2020] and Srinivasan [2017] quantitatively measure the
decentralization of governance using various proxies, while Gervais et al. [2014] and Gu et al. [2020]
qualitatively analyze the governance decentralization of blockchain and blockchain applications.

2.1.5 Transaction Decentralization. The measurement of transaction decentralization is largely
absent in the current blockchain literature. In traditional financial markets, trades are executed in
a single market clearing usually controlled by a centralized market maker. Malamud and Rostek
[2017] describe transactions as "decentralized" when all financial assets in a given market are traded

in multiple coexisting and interconnected trading venues. Chu and Wang [2018], Cong et al. [2022]
characterize transaction decentralization as the proportion of transactions conducted by the top
nodes in value transacted. Similarly, we define transaction decentralization as the evenness in
the distribution of transactions across users of a blockchain. This definition further describes the
extent of distributed usage among different parties. Transaction decentralization thus describes
the decentralization of peer-to-peer transactions on the blockchain, especially examining the
distribution of usage. Cong et al. [2022] further explore transaction decentralization of blockchain
networks using utilization and congestion metrics in combination with the network structure
analysis. De Collibus et al. [2021] examine the concentration of blockchain transaction networks as
growth occurs through super-linear preferential attachment, a phenomenon where new nodes of a
network have a higher probability of linking with more well-connected nodes, causing a few nodes
to become hubs quickly.

2.2 A Review of the Literature on Decentralization
The current blockchain decentralization literature largely studies three aspects of decentralization:
governance, consensus, and wealth. Although by design, blockchains do not restrict who can utilize
the blockchain and validate transactions, the consensus and governance of the blockchain, such
as voting power in validation and upgrades, might be far from decentralized [Wust and Gervais,
2018]. Although blockchain is, by nature, a system with many decentralized entities, the control
and usage of blockchain may not be distributed across many parties. In reality, most blockchain
projects have a â€œfoundationâ€ or community of developers that have disproportionate levels of
influence on decision-making and governing [Pelt et al., 2020]. Bakos et al. [2019] further argues
that blockchains are often subject to a few parties who operate the system. Chen et al. [2020]
assign platforms a â€œdecentralization scoreâ€ based on data collected from CoinCheckup.com and
find that market capitalization often has a U-shaped relationship with governance decentralization,
where the best market performance is associated with a middle level of decentralization. The
decentralization score is assigned according to the state of governance on CoinCheckup.com, with
1 denoting Centralized-Hierarchical, 2 denoting Centralized-Flat, 3 denoting Semi-Centralized, and
4 denoting Decentralized.

Gencer et al. [2018] and Wang et al. [2020] analyze decentralization in Ethereum and Bitcoin
in terms of mining power and bandwidth. Lin et al. [2021] and Srinivasan [2017] instead apply
the Nakamoto coefficient to measure the level of network and consensus decentralization. The
Nakamoto coefficient [Srinivasan, 2017] is defined as the number of entities in a given subsystem
required to reach 51% of the total capacity. Unlike the Gini coefficient, which ranges from 0 to 1,
the Nakamoto coefficient defines a single threshold often considered the number of nodes needed
to compromise a blockchain system. Gochhayat et al. [2020], Kwon et al. [2019] and Wu et al.
[2019] further apply entropy to measure consensus security. Wu et al. [2019] discover that Bitcoin
is 12% more decentralized than Ethereum in terms of mining power and 9% more decentralized
in terms of wealth. Measuring centrality in Bitcoin and Ethereum networks based on consensus
(mining) power, Gochhayat et al. [2020] note that both Bitcoin and Ethereum networks have a
few mining pools that govern the whole network and therefore tend to behave like centralized
systems. Xu et al. [2019] theoretically examines the decentralization of DPoS-based blockchains and
their vulnerabilities. Berman [2018] and Xu et al. [2019] discuss the prevalence of voting collusion
on DPoS-based blockchains that lead to centralization.

Srinivasan [2017] defines wealth decentralization as how evenly assets are distributed on the
native blockchain or application layer. Gochhayat et al. [2020], Gupta and Gupta [2018] and Lin et al.
[2021] apply the Gini coefficient [Gini, 1909] to quantify wealth and consensus decentralization

levels. They discover that Bitcoin [Gupta and Gupta, 2018] and Ethereum [Lin et al., 2021] have
very high levels of wealth concentration.

Governance, consensus, and wealth are all important facets of blockchain decentralization.
However, those aspects can only indirectly evaluate blockchainâ€™s role as a peer-to-peer electronic
payment system [Mohanta et al., 2018, Nguyen, 2016, Tasatanattakool and Techapanupreeda,
2018]. Our taxonomy identifies that a direct measurement of transaction decentralization is still
lacking. Next, our research applies entropy to quantify transaction decentralization, an aspect of
decentralization missing in the literature.

4.2 Descriptive Analysis: Decentralization Index in DeFi
To compare the level of decentralization across different tokens and DeFi applications, we create
boxplots, histograms, and time-series diagrams. Each instance represents the decentralization index
value with a window period of 24 hours. The boxplots show the relative levels of decentralization
for different applications and application categories. The histograms elaborate on the distribution
of daily decentralization levels within categories. The time-series diagram shows the changes in
decentralization values over time, capturing trends and fluctuations due to market dynamics, and
aims to illustrate how decentralization is affected by real-world events.

Figure 3 shows that in general, DEX and lending applications have greater decentralization
than payment and derivative applications. This is, in part, due to the number of transactions. DEX
and lending applications have, by and large, more daily transactions because the market has a
higher demand for exchanging and borrowing than other motives. Figure 4 shows that the level of
decentralization of tokens within each category varies greatly: tokens with a higher TVL ranking
tend to be more decentralized. This again shows that tokens with greater market demand and
utilization tend to be more decentralized.

In Figure 5, we note that Uniswap and Sushiswap have different starting points than Balancer
and Bancor because the former two projects airdropped their native tokens to loads of early users.
Despite the very decentralized start, these projects tend to revert to being more centralized and
attain a similar level of decentralization over time. Notably, Campajola et al. [2022] document
a similar shift towards greater centralization of transactions measured by the average number
of addresses per entity, with a few entities participating in a large number of transactions. Ao
et al. [2022] also note an intertemporal trend of centralization using social network analysis on
the DeFi token of AAVE, where transactions have become more centralized over time despite an
initial decentralized structure. One potential explanation is that most of those who obtained the
airdropped tokens will eventually sell their tokens, and tokens will be accumulated by the whale
holders. That is, regardless of the initial allocation, the token distribution will converge to a similar
level. This phenomenon holds for lending applications as well (see Figure 6). Cong et al. [2022]
corroborates with our results, observing that there is a shift from peer-to-peer interactions to more
transactions by large players. The mechanism behind is interesting to explore further; we leave it
for future research.

4.4 Causal Inference: EIP-1559
EIP-1559 is a major overhaul of the transaction fee mechanism (TFM) for the Ethereum blockchain [Bu-
terin et al., 2019]. EIP-1559 makes Ethereum the first major blockchain to move away from the
classic first-price auction mechanism in coordinating the demand and supply of transactions. On
August 5th, 2021, Ethereum activated the London Hard-fork, bringing major changes to the TFM
by adding a base fee parameter and how users specify the bids for transaction fees. 4 Users can now
bid the maximum priority charge per gas and the maximum fee per gas in their transactions. Users
tip miners to prioritize their transactions using priority fees per gas unit. The maximum fee per gas
parameter acts as a cap on both the base and priority fees. Liu et al. [2022a] evidence the positive
impacts of EIP-1559 on user experience by simplifying the fee estimation process, reducing the
difference in gas price paid between blocks, and decreasing waiting times. These changes smoothen
the transaction process, making peer-to-peer transactions easier and swifter. In this section, we
seek the causal impacts of EIP-1559 on transaction decentralization. Our study further connects
economic factors to transaction decentralization in cases of mechanism changes.

4.4.1 Methodology. One of the most difficult challenges in determining the causal effect of EIP-1559
on blockchain decentralization is distinguishing the effect of EIP-1559 from confounding factors
such as price, volatility, market movement, and time trend. We adopt Regression Discontinuity
Design (RDD) [Athey and Imbens, 2017, Imbens and Lemieux, 2008, Lee and Lemieux, 2010]
to analyse the effects of EIP-1559 on transaction decentralization. RDD is a quasi-experimental
evaluation frequently used in economics, political science, epidemiology, and related fields. We
use the London Hardfork, the days from the EIP-1559 London Hardfork, and their interaction as
the independent variables in the RDD framework to estimate the immediate effects of the London
Hardfork and the average treatment effects of EIP-1559 adoption. Liu et al. [2022a] adopted a similar
framework to analyze the impact of EIP-1559 on various transaction-related economic factors,
including waiting times and gas prices, while [Cong et al., 2022] also uses RDD to analyze the
impact of EIP-1559 on mining reward distribution and transaction volume.

5 DISCUSSION
Blockchain creates a system of decentralized trust that relies on economic incentives to secure
the system by ensuring the benefits of dishonesty are outweighed by the costs [Budish, 2018].
Decentralization plays a key role in balancing the economic incentives that secure a blockchain
from majority attacks. Weyl et al. [2022] envisions a decentralized society where commitments,
credentials, and affiliations are encoded through non-transferable â€œsoul-boundâ€ tokens (SBTs)
maintained on blockchain that creates a trusted network of the real economy. Decentralization lies
at the core of what blockchain promises.

We make three major contributions to blockchain decentralization. First, our study systematically
reviews various aspects of blockchain decentralization and constructs a taxonomy. We categorize
current studies by aspects of decentralization measured and summarize the methods of measure-
ment. This approach lays a foundation for future research and facilitates the design of blockchain
mechanisms that enable decentralization. For example, it is unclear how the different dimensions
of decentralization impact each other in a blockchain system. The mechanics and incentives that
motivate decentralized usage are largely unknown.

Second, we contribute to the literature gap by proposing a new index that intuitively characterizes
transaction decentralization. Transaction decentralization is an essential aspect of blockchain
decentralization. On the one hand, the decentralized transaction intuitively characterizes the usage
of blockchain systems in the spirit of peer-to-peer (P2P) networks with financial inclusions. On the
other hand, a blockchain with centralized transactions is highly manipulable by a small number
of prominent players who effectively control the market force and heavily influence prices. For
instance, the Ampleforth stablecoin adjusts supplies, responding to price deviations from the target
price [Zhang and Liu, 2021]. Thus, transaction decentralization is also critical to the security of the
blockchain ecosystem that hosts smart contracts. Through empirical data analysis, we establish
that our decentralization index is a new technical indicator of market dynamics that incorporates
information beyond traditional measures, such as transaction volume. Additionally, we show that
our decentralization index is associated with economic factors. We pave the way for further analysis
on the interactions of decentralization and economic factors.

Last, our study contributes to the design of blockchain mechanisms. Although blockchains are
decentralized P2P systems by design, the realized level of decentralization varies substantially
in reality. We observe that the level of decentralization might converge despite the difference in
initial allocations of assets and technique designs. The convergence, unexplained by the literature,
might be driven by economic incentives and market forces. Our results thus also shed light on
a new direction of blockchain mechanism design that investigates both the origin and the long-
term equilibrium of the level of decentralization. For instance, Figure 9 represents the time series
of the decentralization index comparing the fiat-backed stablecoin USDt and the crypto-backed
stablecoin Sai and dai. USDt is much more centralized for consensus. But ironically, USDt is much
more decentralized at the transaction layer. What causes the discrepancy in decentralization levels
between consensus and transaction? We leave the question for future research.
