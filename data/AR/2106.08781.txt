1. Introduction

Solar Ô¨Çares are powerful explosive events observed in the so-
lar atmosphere that often occur in association with coronal mass
ejections (CMEs). During Ô¨Çares, a vast amount of magnetic en-
ergy is released, converted into particle acceleration, kinetic en-
ergy of mass ejections, plasma heating, and leading to enhanced
emission of radiation across the electromagnetic spectrum (see
reviews by Fletcher et al. 2011; Benz 2017). The underlying
physical process involves accumulation of free magnetic en-
ergy in the corona by induced electric currents, which is even-
tually released by magnetic reconnection (for a review see, e.g.,
Priest & Forbes 2002; Shibata & Magara 2011). While smaller
Ô¨Çare events tend to be conÔ¨Åned in nature (i.e., are not accom-
panied by a CME), large Ô¨Çares mostly show an eruptive char-
acter (i.e., they are associated with a CME; see, e.g., Svestka
& Cliver 1992; Andrews 2003; Yashiro et al. 2005). CMEs
carry plasma and embedded magnetic Ô¨Åeld into the interplane-
tary space and can cause major geomagnetic storms upon inter-
action with Earth's magnetosphere (e.g., Tsurutani et al. 1988;
Koskinen et al. 2017).

Over the last two decades, many attempts have been under-
taken to forecast Ô¨Çares. Some of these Ô¨Çare prediction methods
rely on the characterization of distinct features at photospheric
levels, based on observed magnetic Ô¨Åeld or intensity images,

within the host active region (AR). Other so-called statistics-
based approaches utilize information on prior Ô¨Çare activity only.
Barnes et al. (2016) systematically compared such Ô¨Çare predic-
tion methods and showed that the diÔ¨Äerent approaches have a
comparable Ô¨Çare prediction ability but require further improve-
ment. Leka & Barnes (2003, 2007) concluded in their study that
the information contained in the photospheric magnetic Ô¨Åeld at
any particular time is limited. This is because the parameters de-
duced from the 2D imprint of the intrinsically 3D coronal mag-
netic Ô¨Åeld, underestimate essential aspects which contribute to a
Ô¨Çare process (see also, e.g., Barnes & Leka 2006).

SigniÔ¨Åcant progress has also been made in understanding the
physical requirements for the occurrence of CMEs. DiÔ¨Äerent ap-
proaches have been developed based on observations or model-
ing to assess whether a Ô¨Çare is likely to be eruptive (i.e., CME-
associated) or conÔ¨Åned (for a review on CME prediction meth-
ods, see Sect. 3 in Green et al. 2018). Wang & Zhang (2007)
examined 14 X-class Ô¨Çares and found that the location of a Ô¨Çare
within the host AR plays a signiÔ¨Åcant role. In particular, con-
Ô¨Åned large Ô¨Çares tend to originate from locations closer to the
AR center, while eruptive Ô¨Çares more likely originate from the
AR periphery. Baumgartner et al. (2018) analyzed 44 Ô¨Çares of
GOES class (cid:62) M5.0 and also noted that conÔ¨Åned large Ô¨Çares pri-
marily originate from locations close to the Ô¨Çux-weighted center

Concerning the coronal pre-requisites for Ô¨Çare productivity,
observation-based 3D modeling of the coronal magnetic Ô¨Åeld de-
livered important insights. The free magnetic energy previously
stored in the AR corona appears to be related to the frequency
and size (magnitude) of upcoming Ô¨Çares (e.g., Jing et al. 2010;
Su et al. 2014). A certain pre-Ô¨Çare free magnetic energy con-
tent, however, is not decisive whether a Ô¨Çare will occur, as not
necessarily all of the coronal free magnetic energy is released at
once during a Ô¨Çare (e.g., Gilchrist et al. 2012). These Ô¨Åndings
suggest that dissipative quantities, such as the magnetic Ô¨Çux or
the free magnetic energy, have a limited ability for Ô¨Çare predic-
tion, as they are not uniquely related to the complexity of the
Ô¨Çare-involved magnetic Ô¨Åeld. In this respect, magnetic helicity
has a number of advantageous properties, as it depends not only
on the strength but is also unique to the structure of the underly-
ing magnetic Ô¨Åeld. In addition, contrary to magnetic energy, it is
very well conserved even in non-ideal dynamics Berger (1984).
Tziotziou et al. (2012) found a robust correlation between the
free magnetic energy and the magnetic helicity in Ô¨Çaring ARs,
and suggested that magnetic helicity represent an essential in-
gredient in addition to free energy for large solar eruptions. Park
et al. (2008) noted that the coronal magnetic helicity accumu-
lates at a nearly constant rate, and stagnates prior to the onset of
individual Ô¨Çares. Therefore, studying the time variations of the
magnetic helicity and the free energy can help us to better under-
stand the evolution of the active region corona, and the process
leading to a Ô¨Çare and CME.

Magnetic helicity is a measure of geometrical complexity
of the magnetic Ô¨Åelds, summing the individual contributions of
twist and writhe, as well as the interlinking of magnetic struc-
tures (MoÔ¨Äatt 1969). It is a conserved quantity of ideal magneto-
hydrodynamics (MHD), and almost conserved in resistive MHD
(Taylor (1974); Berger & Field (1984); Pariat et al. (2015)). The
basic deÔ¨Ånition of magnetic helicity is gauge invariant only for
magnetically closed systems, i.e., where no Ô¨Åeld threads the con-
sidered volume‚Äôs boundary. For applications to open systems,
such as the solar corona, Berger & Field (1984) and Finn & An-
tonsen (1985) formulated a relative measure for the total helicity
within a considered volume, V, in the form,

Based on these exemplary case studies, it appears that the he-

produce large eruptive Ô¨Çares from ARs that produce mostly con-
Ô¨Åned Ô¨Çares. This motivates us to perform a Ô¨Årst comprehensive
study, including a larger number of ARs, and to analyze in detail

and larger). In particular, we are interested in the coronal char-

(eruptive or conÔ¨Åned) and investigate the evolution of helicity
and energy-based proxies and test their ability to indicate the
eruptive potential of solar ARs.

2.2. Magnetic Ô¨Åeld modeling and helicity computation

The application of Eq. (1) to the solar corona is hampered by
several diÔ¨Éculties, most severely by our inability to measure
the coronal magnetic Ô¨Åeld reliably on a routine basis (e.g., re-
views by Cargill 2009; Wiegelmann et al. 2014). Therefore, the
coronal magnetic Ô¨Åeld is typically approximated by a nonlinear
force-free (NLFF) Ô¨Åeld within a Ô¨Ånite volume, which requires
the measured surface vector magnetic Ô¨Åeld as an input (see re-
views by Wiegelmann & Sakurai 2012; Wiegelmann et al. 2017).
The particular approach used in our study is an optimiza-
tion method, applied to appropriate boundary conditions (de-
duced from observed photospheric vector magnetic Ô¨Åeld mea-
surements) to numerically iterate for an approximate solution to
the force-free equations, j √ó B = 0 and ‚àá ¬∑ B = 0, within a chosen
computational domain. We apply the method of Wiegelmann &
Inhester (2010) using the 12-min cadence hmi.sharp_720s data,
constructed from polarization measurements of the Helioseismic
and Magnetic Imager (HMI; Scherrer et al. 2012) onboard the
Solar Dynamics Observatory (SDO; Pesnell et al. 2012). The
data set is binned by a factor of two to a spatial resolution of
0.06 degrees (‚àº720 km at the disk center).

The magnetic Ô¨Åeld modeling involves two computational
tasks. First, a preprocessing of the observed vector magnetic
Ô¨Åeld data (Wiegelmann et al. 2006) is required in order to obtain
a force-free consistent bottom boundary. Second, based on the
preprocessed boundary data, the NLFF corona above the AR is
approximated by applying a combination of the improved opti-
mization scheme of Wiegelmann & Inhester (2010) and a multi-
scale approach (Wiegelmann et al. 2008).

In a recent study, Thalmann et al. (2020) investigated the ef-
fect of particular choices of free model parameters, both dur-
ing preprocessing and optimization, onto subsequent magnetic
helicity computation. It was found in their study that the appli-
cation of preprocessing with default parameters (including the
smoothing of the originally observed vector data) improves the
NLFF model quality, in terms of force-freeness and solenoidal-
ity (see also Wiegelmann et al. 2006). It was also shown that
further improvement of the solenoidality of the NLFF models
can be achieved by enhancing the relative importance of the
volume-integrated divergence compared to the importance of the
volume-integrated Lorentz force, minimized during optimiza-
tion. Therefore, in our study, we analyze two time series of NLFF
models for each AR, one time series of equally strong weighting
of the 3D force- and divergence freeness (w f = wd = 1; see Eq.
(4) in Wiegelmann & Inhester 2010), and one time series with
an enhanced weighting of the volume-integrated divergence (on
the expense of force-freeness; w f = 1, wd = 2).

To determine the force-free consistency of the NLFF models,
we calculate the current-weighted angle between the modeled
magnetic Ô¨Åeld and the electric current density, Œ∏J (e.g., Schri-
jver et al. 2006). For a completely force-free Ô¨Åeld, Œ∏J = 0. We
note here that the average Œ∏J in our NLFF models, for all of the
considered ARs and most times during the analysis time inter-
vals, is (cid:46) 15‚ó¶ (see Fig. (A.1) in the Appendix A). In order to
determine the degree of solenoidality of a given B, Valori et al.
(2013) introduced a relative measure which expresses the frac-
tion of the total magnetic energy (E) that is related to the non-
zero divergence (Ediv), in the form Ediv/E. For completeness,
we show the corresponding values for each AR in Fig. (A.2) in
the Appendix A. Dedicated studies showed that a maximum of
Ediv/E = 0.1 is to be tolerated for subsequent magnetic helicity
computation (Valori et al. 2016; Thalmann et al. 2019a; Moraitis
et al. 2019). Correspondingly, we analyze the coronal magnetic

4. Discussion

We investigated the magnetic helicity and energy budgets of ten
Ô¨Çare-productive active regions around the time of occurrence of
large Ô¨Çares (GOES class M1.8-X9.3). Our goal was to identify
characteristic pre-Ô¨Çare magnetic energy and helicity budgets and
to inquire regarding a possible relation to the type of Ô¨Çare pro-

duced (conÔ¨Åned or eruptive). Thus, we computed relative mag-
netic helicities by applying a Ô¨Ånite volume method. The helic-
ity and energy computations are based on 3D coronal magnetic
Ô¨Åelds that are reconstructed based on a nonlinear force-free ap-
proach (see Sect. 2.2 for details).

5. Summary and Conclusions

We performed a Ô¨Årst comprehensive study of a sample of ten
ARs, for a detailed analysis of the time evolution of the coro-
nal magnetic energy and helicity around the time of large Ô¨Çares
(GOES class ‚â•M1.0). We computed the relative magnetic he-
licity using the Ô¨Ånite volume method of Thalmann et al. (2011)
based on NLFF modeling (Wiegelmann & Inhester 2010). Our
major Ô¨Åndings are as follows:

i) The extensive quantities (E, EF, HV , HJ, HPJ) cover a broad
range of magnitudes with their pre-Ô¨Çare levels, being not
discriminative regarding the eruptive potential of the Ô¨Çare-
producing AR.

ii) Among the intensive quantities, |HV |/œÜ(cid:48)2, is not distinctly
diÔ¨Äerent for ARs that produce an eruptive or conÔ¨Åned Ô¨Çare.
However, the other intensive quantities, such as the helicity
ratio (|HJ|/|HV |), the energy ratio (EF/E) and the normalized
non-potential helicity (|HJ|/œÜ(cid:48)2) are able to distinguish the
pre-Ô¨Çare corona of ARs that possibly produce a large erup-
tive or conÔ¨Åned Ô¨Çare. We Ô¨Ånd characteristic pre-Ô¨Çare val-
ues of (cid:104)|HJ|/|HV |(cid:105) > 0.1, (cid:104)EF/E(cid:105) > 0.2, and (cid:104)|HJ|/œÜ(cid:48)2(cid:105) >
0.005 for ARs that produce large eruptive Ô¨Çares. For ARs
that produce mostly large Ô¨Çares that are conÔ¨Åned, we Ô¨Ånd
(cid:104)|HJ|/|HV |(cid:105) (cid:46) 0.1, (cid:104)EF/E(cid:105) (cid:46) 0.1, and (cid:104)|HJ|/œÜ(cid:48)2(cid:105) (cid:46) 0.002.
iii) All helicity related intensive quantities studied here show
a pronounced response during the Ô¨Çare if it was associated
with a CME.

These major Ô¨Åndings support that the combined analysis of
intensive quantities (energy ratio and helicity ratio) allows it to
successfully characterize the potential of an AR to produce a
CME. Such an analysis, however, does not allow it to unambigu-

Ô¨Çare, i.e., whether or not the next occurring Ô¨Çare will be erup-
