The generation and transformation of polarisation signals in molecular
lines through collective anisotropic resonant scattering

ABSTRACT
We discuss the existence of elliptical polarisation in rotational spectral lines of CO and other molecules within the context of the
Anisotropic Resonant Scattering (ARS) model. We show that the eÔ¨Äect of ARS on the radiation Ô¨Åeld can lead to not only the
previously predicted transformation of background linear polarisation into circular polarisation (i.e., Faraday conversion), but
also the occurrence of Faraday rotation and the generation of elliptically polarised signals in an otherwise initially unpolarised
radiation Ô¨Åeld. This is due to a collective behaviour between the large number of molecules acting as a diÔ¨Äraction ensemble that
strongly favours forward scattering over any other mode. Our application to astronomical data demonstrates the dependency of
the Stokes parameters on the strength and orientation of the ambient magnetic Ô¨Åeld, and suggests that ARS will manifest itself
for a wide range of molecular species and transitions.

1 INTRODUCTION

Given the challenges involved in acquiring direct measurements of
magnetic Ô¨Åeld strengths through the Zeeman eÔ¨Äect over a broad range
of sources (Crutcher 2012), studies aimed at characterizing the im-
portance and the role of magnetic Ô¨Åelds in the star formation process,
or in other environments of the interstellar medium, have long relied
on indirect statistical methods (Davis 1951; Chandrasekhar & Fermi
1953). Accordingly, tools for the analysis of linear polarisation maps
from dust emission have been developed for pursuing these goals and
have provided much of the observational input in the recent past (see,
e.g., Lazarian & Hoang 2007; Hildebrand et al. 2009; Houde et al.
2009; Soler et al. 2013; Houde et al. 2016).

The prospect and subsequent realisation of dust polarization maps
at very high spatial resolutions with ALMA promised to push these
studies well within the domain of circumstellar disks and, further-
more, help elucidate the process of planet formation. Although some
deÔ¨Ånite progress has been made through corresponding observations
of protostellar systems (see, e.g., Maury et al. 2018; Hull & Zhang
2019), the use of dust polarisation maps at the Ô¨Ånest scales has
been limited by the realisation that self-scattering of dust emission
by larger grains in circumstellar disks can remove the correlation
between the relative orientations of ambient magnetic Ô¨Åelds and
measured linear polarisation (pseudo-)vectors (Kataoka et al. 2015;
Yang et al. 2016). Similarly, strong radiative alignment torques can
set dust grains in a precession about the radiation anisotropy vec-
tor rather than that of the magnetic Ô¨Åeld (see Pattle et al. 2021 and
further references therein). A natural solution to this problem would
thus be to rely on linear polarization maps from molecular spectral

The unavoidable consequence stemming from the work of Hezareh
et al. (2013) is that one should not blindly use linear polarisation mea-
surements from molecular lines when applying statistical techniques
initially developed for dust polarisation maps without properly ac-

one should Ô¨Årst verify if circular polarisation signals are present be-
fore attempting such analyses (see, e.g., Ching et al. 2016). This is
to be kept in mind as more measurements of circular polarisation

2019), while a recent search through the SMA archive suggests that
the presence of circular polarisation signals may be relatively com-
mon for several molecular species/transitions (Chamma et al. 2018).
A further implication arising from these considerations is the ne-
cessity of developing a better understanding of the physical pro-
cess(es) responsible for the existence of these polarisation signals in
molecular lines. Accordingly, in this paper we revisit the Anisotropic
Resonant Scattering (ARS) model of Houde et al. (2013) devel-

sider the eÔ¨Äect of ARS on the state of the radiation Ô¨Åeld, and we show

rotation and the generation of elliptically polarised signals in an oth-
erwise initially unpolarised radiation Ô¨Åeld. We further calculate the
dependency of the Stokes parameters on the strength and orientation
of the ambient magnetic Ô¨Åeld responsible for the alignment of the
molecular population. In Sec. 4 we apply our analysis to astronomical
data originally presented in Houde et al. (2013), while we summarize
our results in Sec. 5. Appendices detailing some calculations can be
found at the end.

We have developed in the previous sections a model for the inter-
action between a group of molecules, aligned under the inÔ¨Çuence
of a local magnetic Ô¨Åeld, and an incident radiation Ô¨Åeld originating
from some background source and propagating towards an observer
far away in the foreground. The temporal evolution of this radia-
tion/matter system happens through the fundamental processes of
absorption, emission and scattering of photons. While our analysis
originates from a starting point similar as that presented by Houde
et al. (2013), i.e., seeking to explain the discovery of circularly po-
larised signals in the CO(ùêΩ = 2 ‚Üí 1) rotational line in Orion KL
through the ARS eÔ¨Äect, our results quantitatively diÔ¨Äer from theirs
on some points. These diÔ¨Äerences stem from two key components in
our new analysis, i.e., the realisation that i) the resonance damping
rate Œì is not set by collisions but by the coherent radiative processes
that determine the lifetimes of the virtual states involved in the scat-
tering process, and ii) the full solution to the problem must involve
an elliptical polarisation basis (as opposed to a linear polarisation
basis) to describe the eigenstates of the quantized radiation Ô¨Åeld at
arbitrary magnetic Ô¨Åeld orientations.

The determination of the resonance damping rate is an integral
part of our solution to the problem and yielded a corresponding
value that is orders of magnitude larger than previously thought for
the Orion KL CO(ùêΩ = 2 ‚Üí 1) data. Accordingly, this implies signif-
icantly smaller correlation length ùêø = ùëê/Œì and quantization volume
ùêø3 for the radiation Ô¨Åeld, as well as a reduced number of molecules
involved in the ARS process. The increased value for Œì also brings
into play the imaginary component Œîùõæ ùëù of the relative phase shift
Œîùúô ùëù(= ŒîùúÇ ùëù + ùëñŒîùõæ ùëù) through which the ARS eÔ¨Äect manifests itself
on the polarisation state of the scattered radiation. More precisely,
this imaginary component Œîùõæ ùëù is responsible for the generation of

polarised signals in an otherwise unpolarised incident radiation Ô¨Åeld
(see Sec. 3.3.1). This eÔ¨Äect was negligible in the analysis of Houde
et al. (2013).

While the use of a linear basis for expressing the radiation Ô¨Åeld
initially revealed the existence of a Faraday conversion responsible
for the transformation of linear polarisation into circular polarisation
(and vice-versa), the elliptical polarisation basis further introduces
a Faraday rotation of linearly polarised signals when the magnetic
Ô¨Åeld is (anti-)aligned with the line of sight, and a mixture of the
two eÔ¨Äects at arbitrary inclination angles. The relative importance in
the accrued phase shift Œîùúô ùëù of the term responsible for the Faraday
rotation also largely compensated for the aforementioned reduced
number of scattering molecules and preserved the strength of the
ARS eÔ¨Äect.

Our calculations and application of the ARS eÔ¨Äect to the Orion
KL CO(ùêΩ = 2 ‚Üí 1) data of Houde et al. (2013) has conÔ¨Årmed that
ARS is potentially unavoidable to explain the polarisation charac-
teristics of some molecular rotational lines. First, the generation of
polarisation signals is an inherent part of the eÔ¨Äect. Although our
example showed the levels of linear and circular polarisation ex-
pected from ARS to be on the order of a fraction of a percent for the
case considered (see Fig. 6), they could easily be signiÔ¨Åcantly higher
with slight variations on some parameters (e.g., smaller inclination
angle, higher gas density, stronger magnetic Ô¨Åeld, lower excitation
temperature, etc.) and brought to levels similar to those expected
for the Goldreich-KylaÔ¨Ås eÔ¨Äect (for linear polarisation; Goldreich &
KylaÔ¨Ås 1981). While ARS exhibits the same 90 deg ambiguity as the
Goldreich-KylaÔ¨Ås eÔ¨Äect for the orientation of the generated linear
polarisation relative to that of the plane of the sky component of
the magnetic Ô¨Åeld, it also displays further ambiguities for the incli-
nation angle (‚ÄòùúÑ‚Äô vs. ‚Äòùúã ‚àí ùúÑ‚Äô) and the strength of the magnetic Ô¨Åeld
(through the sign of Œîùõæ ùëù; see Sec. 3.3.1). Second, ARS is highly
eÔ¨Écient in transforming the polarisation state of background radi-
ation signals through the coupling of the Stokes parameters (Secs.
3.1, 3.2 and 3.3.2). We have considered the case of incident lin-
early polarised signal for the Orion KL CO(ùêΩ = 2 ‚Üí 1) data, which
could stem from either the Goldreich-KylaÔ¨Ås or the ARS eÔ¨Äects in
the background radiation source, and saw that, as mentioned earlier,
a mixture of both Faraday conversion and Faraday rotation was re-
sponsible for the appearance of elliptical polarisation in the scattered
radiation. This transformation property of the ARS is not limited to
linear polarisation signals, however, and applies more generally to
incident elliptical polarisation states.

It is important to note that the appearance of ARS polarisation
signals in molecular rotational lines is entirely due to the Zeeman
broadening of the line proÔ¨Åles. Namely, it is the slight relative dis-
placements in frequency of the ùúé¬± spectral components between
themselves and against the ùúã transition that are the sources of the
relative phase shift Œîùúô ùëù. Accordingly, and as we saw in Sec. 3, the
ARS eÔ¨Äect disappears whenever ùúîùëß ‚Üí 0, i.e., as the magnetic Ô¨Åeld
strength vanishes (see equations (66)-(68)). More precisely, while the
Zeeman eÔ¨Äect commonly discussed in the literature is for emission
(or absorption) processes (Crutcher 2012) the eÔ¨Äect underlying our
analysis can, in fact, be directly attributed to the Zeeman eÔ¨Äect for
scattering. Although it is generally expected that scattering rates will
be negligible compared to those due to emission and absorption pro-
cesses for a single molecule at the wavelengths discussed here, the
collective eÔ¨Äect and the resulting coherence between molecules that
characterise ARS greatly enhance the probability amplitude associ-
ated with forward scattering and renders the corresponding Zeeman
eÔ¨Äect (i.e., for scattering) observable for molecules such as CO (see
below). These molecules/transitions are otherwise unaÔ¨Äected by the

ARS enhancement is not limited to the Zeeman eÔ¨Äect for scattering
but would also apply to other phenomena responsible for the splitting

potential confusion, we will keep referring to the Zeeman eÔ¨Äect as
that which applies to emission or absorption processes and to ARS
for the eÔ¨Äect analysed and discussed in this paper.

Another fundamental diÔ¨Äerence between the ARS and Zeeman ef-
fects concerns the way they establish themselves within a molecular
population. That is, any individual molecule sensitive to the Zee-
man eÔ¨Äect (e.g., CN) within a molecular gas subjected to an ambient
magnetic Ô¨Åeld will experience a Zeeman broadening in its spectral
transitions because of the inÔ¨Çuence of the Zeeman interaction term
on the molecular energy levels. In that sense, the Zeeman eÔ¨Äect is felt
locally by all molecules that form the gas and can be observed as long
as the magnetic Ô¨Åeld is strong enough, the molecular column density
suÔ¨Écient to build a detectable Stokes ùëâ spectrum and velocity gra-
dients or magnetic Ô¨Åeld reversals are not too pronounced to weaken
or cancel Zeeman features along corresponding lines of sight. On
the other hand, ARS is a collective eÔ¨Äect between the constituents
and cannot be described as happening on any given molecule with
the eÔ¨Äect increasing incrementally as the radiation scatters on indi-
vidual molecules while propagating through the gas. In other words,
ARS is not local. Rather, ARS will become detectable whenever
a large enough number of molecules is present within the correla-
tion volume characterising the radiation Ô¨Åeld (ùêø3 in our analysis).
These molecules then act as a diÔ¨Äraction unit that enforces forward
scattering as the dominant process and, as a consequence, renders
ARS coherent in nature. This coherent behaviour from the group

Finally, given the predicted strength of ARS for molecules weakly
sensitive to the Zeeman eÔ¨Äect, such as CO, one may inquire about its
potential importance for other molecules (e.g., CN) used to measure
magnetic Ô¨Åeld strengths through the Zeeman eÔ¨Äect. The Zeeman

a signiÔ¨Åcant complication for the interpretation of the corresponding
measurements. Such instances could only be examined on a case by

ùêø. A precise determination of this length scale would then become
essential.

These considerations and the analysis presented in this paper tes-
tify to the fact that the Ô¨Åeld of research focused on the study of
magnetic Ô¨Åelds through the polarisation characteristics of molecular
rotational lines is becoming increasingly rich in its contents and its
reach. Disentangling the eÔ¨Äects from the diÔ¨Äerent processes poten-

was exempliÔ¨Åed by the work of Hezareh et al. (2013), which we dis-

processes should include spectra of all Stokes parameters as both
linear and circular polarisation signals will hold clues vital for the
successful completion of these studies.
