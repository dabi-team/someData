Introduction

Gas-avalanche detectors, introduced originally for the needs of particle-physics experiments, have
become a prominent subject of research also in a variety of other ï¬elds [1]. To this extent, signiï¬cant
eï¬€orts have been dedicated to the development of single-electron [2, 3] and single-photoelectron
UV-photon gaseous detectors; the latter, mainly in the context of Ring Imaging Cherenkov Counters
(RICH) [4, 5], with gaseous and solid photocathodes. Such gaseous photomultipliers (GPM) [6, 7]
provide a cost-eï¬€ective solution suitable for the coverage of large areas with good spatial and
temporal resolution, and low sensitivity to magnetic ï¬elds. GPM detectors have been developed
also for imaging scintillation and electroluminescence photons in noble gases [8] and liquids [9].

In this context, Gaseous Photodetectors [6] employing Multiwire [10] and Drift [11] Cham-
bers, Multi-step avalanche chambers [12], cascaded GEMs [13â€“15] and THGEM-based [16â€“19]
detectors have been playing an important role in experiments. As demonstrated in [20], the more
recent â€œhybridâ€ CsI-coated THGEM-Micromegas has shown to be an eï¬ƒcient upgrade for the
COMPASS-RICH-I. Furthermore, THGEM-based detectors were demonstrated to have moderate
(sub-millimeter) localization resolution [21] and about 10 nsec time resolution, which comply with
the requirements of most RICH devices. Though, previous studies with THGEM detectors have
shown high gas gains for single-photoelectron detection [17], the latter could be considerably lower
in the presence of intense particle background [22].

In some applications, highly ionizing background environment can result in the formation of
large avalanches, often leading to electric discharge. The latter can damage the readout electronics

2 Single-electron charge spectra in gas-avalanche detectors - theoretical aspects

Electron avalanches in gas develop stochastically; the mean-free-path between successive interac-
tions and, accordingly, the electron energy available, can vary considerably between successive
interactions. This is dictated by the cross sections of the diï¬€erent electron interactions with gas
molecules (elastic and inelastic). As a result, the avalanche size (e.g. detector charge gain) ï¬‚uctu-
ations determine the shape of single-photoelectron charge spectra (see detailed study by Alkhazov
[29]). Thus, these statistical ï¬‚uctuations set a physical limit on the single-photoelectron detec-
tion eï¬ƒciency and localization resolution. Furthermore, large avalanche ï¬‚uctuations increase the
probability for high-charge events, with a potential for occasional discharges.

The shape of the single-electron charge distribution depends on the gas mixture, the reduced
electric ï¬eld (E/p; p being the gas pressure and E the electric ï¬eld), the electron initial momentum
and the distance over which the avalanche develops. Gain ï¬‚uctuations can be described quantita-
tively in terms of the probability Pn(r0, p0) that an electron with initial momentum p0 released at a
position r0 initiates an avalanche resulting in n electrons in the detector.

âˆ¼160 nm UV photons and a 55Fe source emitting 5.9 keV X-ray photons. The H2-lamp pulse rate

6 Summary and discussion

In this work, we investigated single-electron spectra obtained with single-stage and double-stage
RPWELL-based detectors. The goal was to evaluate the potential advantage of their operation in
conditions yielding Polya-like single distributions; the latter result from charge-avalanche saturation
in the RPWELL holes. The detectorsâ€™ response was single-photoelectrons emitted from a CsI

photocathode deposited on the multiplierâ€™s surface. Their performance was studied in various gas
mixtures and electric-ï¬eld settings.

Operating in Ne/5%CH4, the single-stage RPWELL detector reached gains of âˆ¼2.5 Ã— 105 in
a discharge-free mode; the single-electron Polya-like distribution had a ğœƒ-value of âˆ¼0.36. The
double-stage THGEM/RPWELL detector reached gains of âˆ¼2.1 Ã— 106 in stable conditions; the
Polya-like distribution reached a ğœƒ-value of âˆ¼0.6.

Similar trends are shown for the variation of the Polya parameters ğœƒ and f as a function of
gain in both RPWELL detector conï¬gurations. Note, however, that for equal gain, the single-stage
detector yielded more pronounced Polya-like distributions, with higher ğœƒ-value and thus narrower
relative gain variance f . This is expected since in a double-stage conï¬guration, the electric ï¬eld
per stage is lower than in a single-stage one, resulting in a decrease of the ionization probability
(gain ï¬‚uctuations) with respect to other inelastic processes. In addition, avalanche-electron losses
between the two stages result in additional avalanche-size ï¬‚uctuations.

The experimental values of ğœƒ and gain, followed by numerical calculations, yielded the single-
electron detection-eï¬ƒciency values; they are presented as function of the threshold (number of
avalanche-electrons). For a given threshold of 104 electrons, the numerical estimation suggests that

the double-stage one (âˆ¼87.5%), due to the higher ğœƒ value. Also, for the single-stage detector, relative
to an exponential distribution at the same gain, the expected single-electron detection eï¬ƒciency is
about âˆ¼6% higher (91% relative to 86%). However, with the increase of the gain, for the same
threshold, both detectors attain similar eï¬ƒciency values. The double-stage detector yielded an
eï¬ƒciency of âˆ¼97% at a gain of âˆ¼2 Ã— 105, with ğœƒ = 0.3. In the presence of 5.9 keV X-ray, the
maximal achievable stable gain is limited to âˆ¼4 Ã— 105 , with a ğœƒ value of âˆ¼0.38 and 99% detection
eï¬ƒciency.

The amount of quencher added to the carrier gas has a major role in reducing instabilities,
which are mainly due to photon-induced secondary avalanches. It also aï¬€ects the electron-transport
parameters and enhances the extraction eï¬ƒciency of photoelectrons from the photocathode in a
single-photon detector (the matter is out of the scope of this work). We studied the eï¬€ect of the CH4
quencher concentration on the detector properties, gain, ğœƒ and the relative gain variance f . It was
