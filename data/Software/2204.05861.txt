ABSTRACT
Phase curves of asteroids are typically considered to depend solely on the scattering properties of airless particulate surfaces and
the size of the object being studied. In this study, we demonstrate the additional dependence of phase curves on object shape,
rotation pole orientation, and viewing geometry over an apparition. Variations in the phase curve of near-Earth asteroid (159402)
1999 AP10 over its apparition from July 2020 - January 2021 are veriï¬ed to be due to aspect changes over the apparition.
This is achieved through shape modelling of the asteroid and simulation of the phase curve over the apparition. We present
simulations of asteroid phase curves over a range of geometries to understand the potential magnitude of this aspect eï¬€ect, and
under which circumstances it can begin to dominate in the phase curves. This dependence on aspect may introduce signiï¬cant
additional uncertainty in the properties derived from phase curve data. We provide and demonstrate software code to estimate
the aspect-related uncertainty in near-Earth asteroid phase curves through simulation and model ï¬tting of a randomly generated
sample of ellipsoidal asteroid models over the observed viewing geometry. We demonstrate how ignoring this eï¬€ect may lead to
misleading interpretations of the data and underestimation of uncertainties in further studies, such as those in the infrared that
use phase curve derived parameters when ï¬tting physical properties of an asteroid.

1 INTRODUCTION

As the heliocentric distance, geocentric distance, and phase angle
(deï¬ned as the angle between the Earth and Sun position vectors
as viewed from the asteroid) of an asteroid change, the observed
brightness of the asteroid viewed from Earth will change. To make
meaningful comparisons of the brightness of diï¬€erent asteroids, it
is standard practice to convert the apparent magnitude to that at a
distance of 1 au from both the Earth and the Sun, referred to as the
reduced magnitude. The variation of the reduced magnitude of an
asteroid (averaged over any rotational light curve period) with chang-
ing phase angle is known as the phase curve. Phase curves allow us
to predict the apparent magnitude of an asteroid for observation plan-
ning, and gain insight into the physical properties of these objects.
The absolute magnitude (ğ») of an asteroid, deï¬ned as the mean re-
duced magnitude when observed at a phase angle of 0Â°, is related
to the diameter and geometric albedo of the object. The slopes of
phase curves are typically explained solely by the scattering proper-
ties of the material on asteroid surfaces. Models such as the ğ», ğº
(Bowell et al. 1989), ğ», ğº1, ğº2 (Muinonen et al. 2010), and ğ», ğº12
(Muinonen et al. 2010) systems aim to approximate the behaviour
of these scattering properties in a parametric manner. Fitting these
models to photometric data helps us to constrain the absolute magni-
tudes, geometric albedos, and potential taxonomic classiï¬cations of
asteroids (Bowell et al. 1989; Oszkiewicz et al. 2012; PenttilÃ¤ et al.
2016).

5 ESTIMATING ASPECT RELATED UNCERTAINTY IN

PHASE CURVE PARAMETERS

We note in the previous sections that the magnitude of aspect eï¬€ects
is highly dependent on viewing geometry and pole orientation. This
means that a generalised approach to estimate a single aspect-related
uncertainty in all asteroid phase curves is not possible. We have
developed software written in c++ and Python that aims to estimate
this uncertainty for asteroid phase curves on an individual basis
for asteroids for which no information on shape, pole orientation
or spectral type is available. Using the same process outlined in
Section 4.2, one thousand ellipsoidal models are generated with pole
orientations according to the outlined input distributions. The models
are all scaled such that the ï¬‚uxes are equivalent to an object with a
1 km volumetric diameter.

The user provides an input geometry ï¬le containing: the heliocen-
tric range, heliocentric longitude, heliocentric latitude, geocentric
range, geocentric longitude, geocentric latitude, and photometric un-
certainty of the asteroid on each date on which it was observed in
the real phase curve. The uncertainties are used to maintain con-
sistency between constraining the best ï¬t parameters for each sim-
ulation and for the real photometric data. The phase curve of the
object is then simulated over this geometry using both S- and C-type
Hapke scattering parameters, using the model and parameters out-

lined in Section 2. We ï¬t the ğ», ğº parameters to each simulated
phase curve using non-linear least squares ï¬tting implemented in the
SciPy python package (Virtanen et al. 2020). The standard deviation
in each of the ï¬tted phase curve parameters across all 1000 mod-
els is recorded as the aspect-related uncertainty for that parameter,
ğœğ» , aspect & ğœğº, aspect. This process is done for models with both
S- and C-type Hapke scattering parameters. S-type simulations show
greater aspect eï¬€ects due to the increased multiple scattering present
on these surfaces. The standard deviations in the ï¬tted parameters are
recorded for both taxonomies. The mean of these standard deviations
is calculated across the two taxonomies, weighted by the taxonomic
proportion of the NEA population, as the ï¬nal aspect uncertainties.
These uncertainties should then be added in quadrature with any
other uncertainties in the phase curve (e.g. statistical ï¬t uncertain-
ties). The latest release of the code can be accessed and downloaded
from doi:10.5281/zenodo.6062631, where some example input ï¬les
are provided alongside instructions on compiling and running the

the code to only generate shapes that match this criteria. For ob-

suitable. If the spectral type of the asteroid is known, the code can

7 CONCLUSIONS

In this paper, we have investigated the eï¬€ect of aspect changes on the
phase curves of near-Earth asteroids. We have presented an investi-
gation into the variability in the phase curve of near-Earth asteroid

MNRAS 000, 1â€“14 (2022)

(159402) 1999 AP10. The light curves from the PIRATE facility
are combined with data from Palmer Divide Observatory obtained
from ALCDEF, in order to produce a convex shape model and pole
solution. The derived shape, pole, and sidereal rotation period match
expectations from independent data. This model is then used to ver-
ify that the deviations in the phase curve arise from aspect eï¬€ects
instead of random scatter in the data. This is achieved via simulation
of the phase curve over the apparition using the shape model and a
Hapke photometric model, which accurately recreates the variation
in the phase curve. The ï¬‚ux-weighted mean latitude of visible and
illuminated facets on the shape model (the â€˜ï¬‚ux latitudeâ€™) changes
from the southern hemisphere to the northern hemisphere through-
out the apparition. This change in observed hemisphere drives the
aspect-related eï¬€ects in the phase curve due to the diï¬€erence in shape
between the two hemispheres of this asteroid. A large change in ï¬‚ux
latitude may accompany large changes in light curve morphology,
so observed changes in the light curves used to construct a phase
curve may indicate the potential presence of aspect changes due to
changing ï¬‚ux latitude. However, a lack of light curve morphological
changes does not necessarily mean a small change in ï¬‚ux latitude or
observed cross-section, so care must still be taken to consider this
eï¬€ect.

After the detection of these aspect changes in the phase curve of
(159402) 1999 AP10, a theoretical study was undertaken in order
to understand how this eï¬€ect may aï¬€ect the phase curves of other
asteroids. Phase curves are simulated for a wide variety of ellipsoidal
shape models over diï¬€erent viewing geometries, demonstrating some
of the worst-case scenarios that may arise. A representative sample of
asteroid models is simulated over the 2020 apparition of (19764) 2000
NF5, displaying the strong dependence of aspect related modulations
to the phase curve on the rotation pole orientation and change in ï¬‚ux
latitude.

Aspect eï¬€ects introduce a systematic uncertainty in the ğ» and ğº
phase curve parameters ï¬t to the data. These additional uncertainties
are highly dependent on speciï¬c viewing geometry, so care must
be taken to model the potential impact of this eï¬€ect for each object
when using parameters derived from their phase curves in further
studies. Taxonomic classiï¬cation using phase curve data is deemed
unreliable from phase curves without supplemental data to support
the classiï¬cation. We provide a program for estimating the additional
uncertainty introduced into estimations of ğ» and ğº when the shape
and pole of the object is unknown.

We have discussed the eï¬€ect of this additional aspect uncertainty
on wider studies of individual asteroids where convex inversion and
extraction of the surface scattering properties directly is not feasible.
This can propagate through to estimations of the diameter, aï¬€ecting
Yarkovsky and YORP eï¬€ect modelling. We have shown how the
additional aspect uncertainty in the phase curve parameters can aï¬€ect
derivations of asteroid properties such as the geometric albedo, and
also how this aï¬€ects our ability to properly separate reï¬‚ected and
emitted ï¬‚uxes in observations of asteroids in the infrared transition
region.

This study has considered this eï¬€ect over a wide variety of convex
shapes. However, real asteroid shapes may have signiï¬cant concavi-
ties and the aspect eï¬€ects may be over or underestimated still by these
methods. The information from non-convex shapes is typically lost
when rotationally averaging for phase curves, however. It remains
the subject of future work to determine the signiï¬cance that large
concavities may have on this eï¬€ect.
