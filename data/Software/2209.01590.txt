1. Introduction

Fish swimming has been an attractive research topic for many years due
to its interdisciplinary nature, involving hydrodynamics (Triantafyllou et al.,
2000; Liao, 2007), schooling (Ashraf et al., 2017), swimming styles (Webb,
1984), muscle anatomy (Altringham and Ellerby, 1999), physiology (Hunt
von Herbing, 2002) and sensory (Liu et al., 2016). The research has been
driven by the Ô¨Åsh farming (Webb and Cotel, 2011) and by biomimetic under-
water robotics (Duraisamy et al., 2019; Fish, 2020). Ô¨Åsh schooling and Ô¨Åsh
swimming style are two interesting sub-categories among the Ô¨Åsh swimming
problems. Multiple factors can inÔ¨Çuence the evolved schooling behaviour
and swimming style, including hydrodynamic eÔ¨Äectiveness, predator defence,
feeding, etc. Body-caudal Ô¨Ån is one of the most common swimming styles
among Ô¨Åsh species (Webb, 1984), with typical examples of its sub-swimming
forms shown in Fig. 1. In this study, we focus on the eÔ¨Äects of Ô¨Åsh schooling
and BCF swimming styles on the hydrodynamics characteristics using sim-
pliÔ¨Åed physical models. This work is inspired by the biological observation
that schooling behaviour of BCF swimmers is, to our best knowledge, only
found in less wavy swimming forms, e.g. thunniform by tunas (Dai et al.,
2020), whereas schooling behaviour is undiscovered in more wavy swimming
forms, e.g. anguilliform by eels.

Fish schooling has been extensively studied in the past decades for its
hydrodynamic characteristics (Weihs, 1973, 1975). Cross sections of two
schooling Ô¨Åsh can be represented by two vibrating cylinders immersed in
quasi-static Ô¨Çuid; it has been identiÔ¨Åed by theoretical (Lamb, 1932; Nair and
Kanso, 2007) and numerical (Gazzola et al., 2012; Lin et al., 2018b,a, 2019)
methods that non-dimensional parameters such as phase diÔ¨Äerence can have
a distinct impact on the Ô¨Çow-mediated interaction between the two cylin-

schooling behaviour. Many hydrodynamic studies have focused on the min-
imal school composed of 2 identical BCF swimmers, using robotic Ô¨Åsh (Li
et al., 2020), biological Ô¨Åsh (Ashraf et al., 2016; Li et al., 2020), hydrofoil

the latter mode being more favourable; lateral and front-back distances are

pattern is more eÔ¨Écient (Weihs, 1973). In the present work, we set the front-
back and lateral distances in a range similar to the previous works (Ashraf
et al., 2016, 2017; Li et al., 2020).

Fish swimming style is a curious topic that has fascinated many re-
searchers (Sfakiotakis et al., 1999; Tytell et al., 2010; Cui et al., 2018; Thekkethil
et al., 2017, 2018, 2020). Sfakiotakis et al. (1999) categorised Ô¨Åsh swimming
styles into several classes, among which the most common one is the body-
caudal Ô¨Ån (BCF) swimming style, featuring the body/tail undulation as the
main propulsion generator. BCF styles can be further divided into four types:
anguilliform, sub-carangiform, carangiform, and thunniform, as exempliÔ¨Åed
in Fig. 1. Thekkethil et al. (2017, 2018, 2020) simpliÔ¨Åed these BCF swim-
mers into undulating/pitching NACA0012 hydrofoils, representing diÔ¨Äerent
swimming forms by non-dimensional wavelength Œª‚àó = Œª/C, where Œª is the
swimming undulation wavelength and C is the Ô¨Åsh chord length. For exam-
ple, anguilliform is typically represented by low wavelength Œª‚àó < 1, whereas
the characteristics of thunniform swimming can be captured by high wave-
length Œª‚àó (cid:29) 1. Thekkethil et al. (2018) discovered that low wavelength Œª‚àó
swimmers generate thrust force by the pressure diÔ¨Äerence between anterior
and posterior body parts, whereas high Œª‚àó transfer streamwise momentum
by pendulum-like motion; small Œª‚àó, e.g. anguilliform swimmers, generally
causes high locomotion eÔ¨Éciency but low thrust production, and vice versa
for large Œª‚àó, e.g. thunniform swimmers. The numerical results obtained by

will be presented later. Nangia et al. (2017a) also discovered that optimal

As schooling and swimming styles of BCF Ô¨Åsh both contain signiÔ¨Åcant
hydrodynamic implications, they can have a combined eÔ¨Äect on the hydro-
dynamic characteristics of underwater swimmers. To our best knowledge,
the systematic biological discussion does not exist on the behavioural corre-
lation between schooling and swimming styles. However, while the schooling
phenomenon is reported for swimmers of sub-carangiform (Trevorrow, 1998),
carangiform (Axelsen et al., 2001; Guillard et al., 2006; Hemelrijk et al., 2010)
and thunniform (Dai et al., 2020; Mitsunaga et al., 2013; Uranga et al., 2019),
anguilliform species seem never found to exhibit schooling behaviour in the
wild. Although hydrodynamics may not be the only factor aÔ¨Äecting Ô¨Åsh‚Äôs
schooling tendency, it is reasonable to hypothesise that sub-carangiform,
carangiform, and thunniform styles are more suited for schooling than an-
guilliform styles from a hydrodynamic perspective.

To justify our numerical methodology, although robotic Ô¨Åsh experiments
could be an eÔ¨Écient way (Li et al., 2020) to study Ô¨Åsh schooling with a
Ô¨Åxed swimming style, it will be time-consuming to design and manufacture
robotic Ô¨Åsh with variable swimming styles. To our best knowledge, exist-
ing robotic Ô¨Åsh studies have not yet involved such a comparison between
swimming styles/wavelengths. For this reason, computational Ô¨Çuid dynam-
ics is utilised to simulate various Ô¨Åsh swimming styles using a representative
problem setup, thanks to its convenience in varying the swimming style by
a uniÔ¨Åed kinematic formula and the capacity to analyse the Ô¨Çow-mediated
interaction mechanism in detail (Thekkethil et al., 2018).

2. Methodology

This section describes the numerical methodology to study the combined
eÔ¨Äects of Ô¨Åsh schooling and swimming styles upon propulsive hydrodynam-
ics. This problem is represented by two identical wavy hydrofoils tethered
in a free-stream Ô¨Çow, as presented in Section 2.1. Numerical simulation is
then executed by IBAMR (GriÔ¨Éth, 2013), an extensively-validated immersed
boundary software, as discussed in Section 2.2. The kinematic model of hy-
drofoil undulation is formulated by the classic travelling wave equation with
additional consideration upon the wavelengths as shown in Section 2.3. Di-
mensional analysis is conducted in Section 2.4 to formalise the investigated
problem. Mesh convergence study and validation can be found in Section 2.5.

2.1. Physical problem setup

In this paper, the two undulating rigid NACA0012 hydrofoils are Ô¨Åxed at
their initial locations, i.e. the foils are "tethered". The non-dimensional form
of the physical problem investigated in the present paper is demonstrated in
Fig. 2. Here, C = 1 is the chord length of the two NACA0012 hydrofoils. G
and D are the lateral and front-back distances between the two hydrofoils,
